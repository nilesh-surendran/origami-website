Camel

  1. Their humps let them store up to 80 pounds of fat which they can live off for weeks and even months!
  2. Camels are very strong and can carry up to 900 pounds for 25 miles a day.
  3. Camels have thick lips which let them forage for thorny plants other animals can’t eat.

Chameleon

  1. they can move their eyes separately, with each eye having a field of 180 degrees. This means they can see 360 degrees if they need to!
  2. 59 unique species of chameleon live on the island of Madagascar and are found nowhere else.
  3. Their tongues consist of both muscles and bones, and can bend round to grab their pray.

Cranes

  1. Cranes are powerful flyers, with some having the ability to fly over the Himalayas.
  2. Cranes are found on all continents except in South America and Antarctica.
  3. They live in wetlands and grasslands habitats.

Pigeon

  1. Pigeons are renowned for their outstanding navigational abilities. They use a range of skills, such as using the sun as a guide and an internal ‘magnetic compass’.
  2. Pigeons mate for life, and tend to raise two chicks at the same time.
  3. Pigeons are highly sociable animals. They will often be seen in flocks of 20-30 birds.

Bear

  1. Grizzly bears possess a biting force of over 1200 PSI, which is enough to crush a bowling ball or an iron skillet.
  2. Adult grizzly bears can run up to an incredible 40mph
  3. There are eight different species of bear: North American black bears, brown bears, polar bears, Asiatic black bears , Andean bears, panda bears, sloth bears, and sun bears.

Panda

  1. Like domestic cats, giant panda bears have vertical slits for pupils.
  2. Giant pandas spend 10-16 hours a day feeding, mainly on bamboo.
  3. Giant pandas are good swimmers and excellent tree climbers.

Flying Cicada

  1. Cicadas can survive a huge fall as babies, or nymphs. They are about the size of a grain of rice when they drop from a tree branch to the ground and start digging.
  2. Most have red-orange eyes. But occasionally cicadas have blue, white or grayish eyes.
  3. They improve lawns by digging tunnels that allow air into the soil. When they die, their rotting bodies put nitrogen into the soil.

Rhino

  1. Sumatran rhinos are the smallest, but they can still weigh 600kg. And white rhinos are the largest, weighing up to 3,500kg
  2. Male rhinos are called 'bulls' and females are called 'cows'. Their young are ‘calves’.
  3. Rhino horn is made up of keratin - the same protein which forms the basis of our hair and nails.
